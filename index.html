About Me

I am currently a software engineer at SeedFi. I studied Electrical and Computer Engineering (Bachelor's May 2020) and Systems Engineering (MEng Fall 2020) at Cornell University.

I was born and raised in Manhattan Beach, California, a small, beachy suburb of Los Angeles. From a young age, I have loved going to the beach and being outside. I am also passionate about sustainability and music. I have played various brass and woodwind instruments throughout my life; notably, in the summer of 2019 I played baritone with the Blue Stars Drum and Bugle Corps.

Experience

Northrop Grumman Corporation

During the Summer of 2018, I interned at the Northrop Grumman Corporation in Redondo Beach, CA. As an intern, I worked with metrology engineers and learned how to perform manual and automated calibrations for lab equipment such as oscilloscopes, power supplies, and digital multimeters. I also calibrated AC/DC standard references and performed virus scans, sanitization and imaging on DC/LF and Radio Frequency measurement and test equipment.

CUSail | Cornell Autonomous Sailboat Team

I am the Full Team Lead of CUSail. CUSail is a 33 member strong, interdisciplinary Cornell Engineering Project Team. We work together as a team to build a fully autonomous robotic sailboat for competition and oceanic research. As the lead, I oversee daily team operations and progress to ensure we are on track for competition in addition to handling team logistics and administration. In the past, I worked on the Mechanical Subteam. I designed the waterproofing systems and the deck and hull of the boat using CAD SolidWorks and eventually manufactuing the parts from composite materials such as fiber glass and carbon fiber. Project Spotlight

This past fall I took a course called Digital System Design Using Microcontrollers. Over the course of the semester, 2 of my peers and I created an air bass guitar that allows the user to play distinct notes by simply moving their fingers without the added weight and cost of an actual bass guitar using a PIC32 microcontroller. The project uses flex sensors, beam break sensors, and an ultrasonic distance sensor to output sound that is accurate both in terms of frequency and duration. Check it out here, featured on Hackaday here!
